## What should be on a packing list for international travel?

A complete packing list for international travel has five sections: documents, carry-on essentials, clothing, toiletries and medications, and tech. Get those five right and the rest is trip-specific extras.

International trips punish forgetfulness more than domestic ones. Forget a charger on a weekend trip and you buy one at a gas station. Forget your passport, prescription meds, or the right power adapter on an international trip and you may not board the plane, or you spend a day of your holiday hunting for a pharmacy that stocks your medication.

This guide is built to be copied. Take the base list, apply one of the destination modifiers at the end, delete what doesn't apply, and you're done. If you want the full pre-departure routine beyond packing — house, money, phone plan — see the [travel checklist before leaving](/blog/travel-checklist-before-leaving/).

## Documents: the section you check twice

Nothing else on this list matters if this section fails. Check it when you write the list and again the night before you fly.

- **Passport** — valid at least 6 months beyond your return date. Many countries refuse entry otherwise.
- **Visa or entry authorization** — check your destination's rules even for short stays. Several regions now require online pre-authorization even for visa-free travelers.
- **Boarding passes** — digital plus a screenshot in case the airline app fails offline.
- **Travel insurance policy** — the policy number and the emergency phone number, accessible without internet.
- **Hotel and booking confirmations** — some border officers ask for proof of accommodation.
- **Driver's license** — plus an International Driving Permit if you plan to rent a car; many countries require it.
- **Copies** — a photo of your passport photo page stored on your phone, and one paper copy packed separately from the original.
- **Payment cards** — two cards from different networks, kept in different bags, plus a small amount of local cash.

Keeping confirmations findable is half the battle. A folder in your email works until you're standing at a reception desk with no signal. Storing boarding passes, reservations, and your insurance policy in one offline place on your phone solves that.

## Carry-on essentials: what never goes in checked luggage

Checked bags get delayed. Plan as if yours will be. The rule: if you can't cheaply replace it at your destination, it flies in the cabin with you.

| Always in carry-on | Why |
|---|---|
| Passport + documents | Cannot be replaced abroad quickly |
| All medications | Pharmacies may not stock or may require local prescriptions |
| Phone, laptop, chargers | Expensive, and lithium batteries must be in the cabin anyway |
| One full change of clothes | Covers a 24-48 hour bag delay |
| Valuables (jewelry, camera) | Theft and damage risk in checked bags |
| Empty water bottle | Fill after security; long flights are dehydrating |
| Pen | Arrival cards still exist in many countries |

Add comfort items to taste: earplugs or noise-canceling headphones, an eye mask, a light sweater (cabins run cold), lip balm, and snacks. Liquids in carry-on must be 100 ml or less per container, in one clear resealable bag — this rule is near-universal internationally.

## The 5-4-3-2-1 clothing formula

Overpacking clothing is the most common packing mistake, and a formula fixes it. For one week:

- **5 tops** — t-shirts, shirts, or blouses that all work with every bottom
- **4 bottoms** — pants, skirts, or shorts suited to the climate
- **3 pairs of shoes** — one comfortable walking pair, one casual, one dressier or activity-specific
- **2 layers** — a sweater or hoodie plus a jacket appropriate to the destination
- **1 nice outfit** — for a dinner, a show, or anywhere with a dress code

Plus underwear and socks for each day (7 of each for a week), sleepwear, and swimwear if there's any chance of water.

Three rules make the formula work:

1. **Pick one or two base colors.** If everything is built around navy and grey, every top matches every bottom and you get 20 outfits from 9 pieces.
2. **Wear the bulkiest items on the plane.** Boots, the heavy jacket, the thickest sweater — on your body, not in your bag.
3. **Going longer than a week? Do laundry, don't pack more.** The 5-4-3-2-1 count covers two weeks with one wash. Almost every destination has laundromats or hotel laundry.

## Toiletries and medications

Toiletries are the easiest category to cut, because nearly everything is available at your destination. Pack the minimum, buy the rest if needed.

**Toiletries:**
- Toothbrush, toothpaste, floss
- Deodorant
- Travel-size shampoo and soap (or rely on the hotel's)
- Razor and shaving needs
- Sunscreen — expensive and sometimes hard to find abroad; bring it
- Skincare basics and any makeup you actually use daily
- Contact lenses, solution, and a backup pair of glasses
- Hairbrush or comb

**Medications — this part is not optional:**
- All prescription medication in original packaging, in your carry-on, with a few days extra in case of delays
- A copy of the prescription for anything controlled or injectable; some countries check
- Painkillers, motion-sickness tablets, anti-diarrheal, antihistamines
- Plasters and blister care — new shoes plus 20,000 steps a day is a predictable combination
- Any destination-specific items your doctor recommends (altitude, malaria zones, required vaccinations documentation)

Check your destination's rules for common medications. Some drugs that are over-the-counter at home are restricted elsewhere.

## Tech and adapters

- **Phone + charger** — the item most often left in a wall socket at home
- **Power adapter** — check your destination's plug type; a universal adapter with USB ports covers everything
- **Power bank** — must be in carry-on, most airlines cap at 100Wh (27,000 mAh)
- **Charging cables** — one spare for your most important device
- **Headphones**
- **E-reader or tablet** — if you'll actually use it
- **Camera + memory cards + charger** — if your phone isn't enough

Two notes. First, most modern chargers accept 100-240V, so you need an adapter (plug shape) but almost never a converter (voltage) — check the fine print on the charger to confirm. Second, sort out data before you fly: an eSIM for your destination or a confirmed roaming plan beats airport Wi-Fi hunting.

## Destination modifiers: adjust the base list

Don't write a new list per trip. Keep the base list and add one block.

**Beach:**
- 2 swimsuits (one dries while you wear the other)
- Flip-flops or sandals
- High-SPF sunscreen and after-sun
- Sun hat and sunglasses
- Dry bag for phone and valuables
- Light cover-up

**City:**
- Your most comfortable walking shoes — this is the whole trip
- Daypack or crossbody bag that zips (pickpockets work every major city)
- Smart-casual layer for restaurants
- Portable umbrella

**Cold weather:**
- Thermal base layers (tops and bottoms)
- Insulated jacket, hat, gloves, scarf
- Waterproof boots with grip
- Wool socks
- Lip balm and heavier moisturizer

**Business:**
- Suit or equivalent, packed in a garment bag or folded over tissue paper
- Dress shoes plus a comfortable pair for transit
- Laptop, presentation backup on a USB stick
- Business cards
- A steamer-friendly fabric choice — or plan to use the hotel iron on day one

## What NOT to pack

Cutting is as important as adding. Leave these at home:

- **"Just in case" outfits.** If you can't name the day you'll wear it, it stays.
- **Full-size toiletries.** Heavy, spill-prone, and replaceable anywhere.
- **More than 3 pairs of shoes.** The heaviest, bulkiest category in any bag.
- **A different bag for every function.** One daypack covers hiking, city walking, and the flight.
- **Books, plural.** One paperback or an e-reader.
- **A towel** — hotels have them; only pack one for camping or hostels that charge.
- **Expensive jewelry.** Risk with no upside.
- **Anything prohibited at your destination.** Check rules on drones, e-cigarettes, and certain medications — each is banned or restricted in some countries.

The test for every borderline item: *can I buy this at my destination for under $10?* If yes, and you're not certain you'll need it, leave it.

## How to actually use this list

A packing list only works if you check things off as they physically enter the bag — not from memory, not "I'm sure I packed it." The reliable routine:

1. **Two weeks out:** write the list, order anything missing (adapter, meds refill).
2. **Three days out:** lay everything out in one place. Cull the "just in case" pile.
3. **The night before:** pack, checking off each item as it goes in.
4. **Before leaving home:** final sweep — wall sockets, bathroom shelf, fridge, door keys.
