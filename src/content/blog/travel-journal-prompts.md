## Why use travel journal prompts at all?

Because the blank page is the reason most travel journals die by day three. You sit down tired after a full day, the page asks "so, what happened?", and the honest answer — *a lot, and I don't know where to start* — sends you to bed instead. A prompt removes that decision. It asks one specific question, you answer it, and the entry exists.

One rule matters more than any prompt on this list: **five minutes a day beats an hour on Sunday**. Details have a short shelf life — the smell of a market, the exact thing the taxi driver said, how the first evening felt — and most of them are gone within 48 hours. A short daily entry written from fresh memory will always beat a long weekly catch-up written from a fading one. Pick one prompt per day, answer it in three to six sentences, and stop. If you want the full method — formats, tools, and how to pair entries with photos — our guide on [how to keep a travel journal](/blog/how-to-keep-a-travel-journal/) covers it end to end.

The 50 prompts below are grouped by when they work: 10 before the trip, 25 during, 15 after.

## 10 travel journal prompts for before the trip

Anticipation is half the trip, and it is the half nobody records. These work in the days or weeks before departure — the answers become the most interesting pages later, because you can hold them up against what actually happened.

1. Why this destination, honestly — what first put it on your list?
2. What do you imagine a typical day there will look like, hour by hour?
3. What are you most looking forward to, and what do you expect it to feel like?
4. What are you nervous or anxious about, however small?
5. What do you already believe about this place, and where did that picture come from?
6. What would make this trip a success — name the one thing?
7. What are you hoping to get a break *from*?
8. Who are you travelling with, and what do you hope the trip does for you as a group?
9. What is one thing you want to do there that is slightly outside your comfort zone?
10. Write a short note to your post-trip self: what do you want them to remember to appreciate?

## 25 travel journal prompts for during the trip

This is the core of the journal, so the prompts are split into five situations you will actually meet on the road. You do not need all 25 — one per day, chosen to fit the day you just had, is the whole job.

### Arrival day

First impressions expire fastest of all — within a day or two the strange becomes normal and you stop noticing it. Write these on day one or lose them.

11. What was the very first thing you noticed stepping off the plane, train, or ferry?
12. What does this place smell and sound like right now?
13. What is the first thing that felt genuinely different from home?
14. Describe the journey from the airport or station to where you are sleeping.
15. How does your room, tent, or apartment compare to what you pictured before you left?

### Food

Meals are the easiest daily anchor a journal can have — there is always one worth writing about, and food memories fade into "it was nice" faster than almost anything else.

16. Describe today's best meal properly: where you sat, what was on the plate, what it actually tasted like.
17. What did you eat today that you had never eaten before?
18. What do locals around you eat and drink that tourists seem to skip?
19. What did the best thing you ate today cost, and was it worth double or half that?
20. Which meal from home do you miss right now, if any?

### People

Trips are remembered as places, but the stories you retell for years are almost always about people. Catch them while the dialogue is still word-for-word in your head.

21. Who did you talk to today, and what did they say that stuck?
22. Describe one stranger you watched today — what do you imagine their day looks like?
23. What has surprised you about how people here treat each other, or treat you?
24. What moment of unexpected kindness or help did you receive today?
25. If you are travelling with someone: what did you learn or notice about them today?

### Places

The trick with places is to skip the guidebook summary and record your own angle — the detail the postcard leaves out is the one that will bring the whole scene back.

26. Describe where you are sitting right now, in enough detail that a stranger could draw it.
27. What did today's main sight actually feel like to stand in — beyond what the guidebook promised?
28. What did you find today that no guidebook or app sent you to?
29. What is one thing about how this place works — transport, shops, queues, mornings — that quietly impressed you?
30. If you could transplant one street, view, or habit from here back home, which one?

### Hard moments

Bad days make the best entries — they carry more story than any sunset, and they are the pages you will laugh at in five years. Write them the same day, while the frustration is still honest.

31. What went wrong today, and what did you do about it?
32. What are you finding harder than you expected — the language, the heat, the pace, each other?
33. What do you miss from home right now, beyond the people?
34. Describe a moment today when you felt lost, awkward, or out of place.
35. What would you tell someone arriving tomorrow to do differently than you did?

## 15 travel journal prompts for after the trip

A journal that just stops at the last hotel never gets reread. These prompts give the trip a closing chapter — do the reflection set in the first week home, and the memory-keeping set whenever you sort your photos.

### Reflection

36. What is the very first story from this trip you told someone after landing?
37. What surprised you most about the place — and what surprised you most about yourself?
38. Which expectation from your pre-trip pages turned out completely wrong?
39. What was the single best hour of the whole trip, and what made it that?
40. What was the low point, and how does it look now with a week's distance?
41. What did the trip actually cost against what you planned — and which overspend was worth every penny?
42. What habit, food, or attitude from the trip do you want to keep at home?
43. How did travelling together change things between you and your companions?
44. What would you do differently if you repeated this exact trip?
45. Has the trip changed what kind of traveller you want to be next time?

### Memory-keeping

46. Pick your ten favourite photos and write one sentence for each — the sentence the photo cannot say by itself.
47. List the small physical souvenirs — tickets, receipts, a beer label — and note where each one was acquired.
48. Write down the three facts you will otherwise forget: names of places, dishes, and people you want to remember exactly.
49. Record the trip's numbers: days, cities, kilometres, best meal's price, books read, photos taken.
50. Write a one-page letter to the next person who takes this trip — even if that person is future you.

Prompt 46 works best if the photos are already in order; our guide on [how to organize travel photos](/blog/how-to-organize-travel-photos/) has a simple system for culling and sorting them before you write. ## How do you actually keep the habit going?

The prompts solve the blank page; these four habits solve everything else.

- **Attach it to an anchor.** Journal at the same trigger every day — with your morning coffee, on the evening train, in bed before sleep. A habit tied to a moment survives; a habit tied to "when I get a chance" does not.
- **Keep entries small on purpose.** Three to six sentences answering one prompt is a complete entry. If you write more, fine — but never *owe* more. The journals that reach the last day of the trip are the modest ones.
- **Never write catch-up entries.** Missed yesterday? Let it go and write today. Reconstructing two lost days from memory feels like homework, produces the journal's worst pages, and is the single most common reason people quit.
- **Keep the journal where the trip already lives.** The less friction between you and the page, the more entries exist. ## Pick ten and go

Do not carry all 50 prompts with you — that is a menu, not a plan. Before you leave, pick around ten that fit your trip: two or three from the pre-trip list this week, one per travel day matched to arrival, food, people, places, or the inevitable hard moment, and three or four for the week you get back. Five minutes a day, one honest answer at a time, and you will come home with the rarest souvenir there is: a trip you can actually reread.
