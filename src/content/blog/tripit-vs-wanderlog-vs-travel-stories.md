## What is the real difference between TripIt, Wanderlog, and Travel Stories?

These three apps get compared constantly, but they were built to solve three different problems.

**TripIt** organises trips you have already booked. Its signature trick is email forwarding: send any confirmation email — flight, hotel, rental car, restaurant — to plans@tripit.com and it parses the details into a chronological itinerary automatically. It is the closest thing to a personal travel assistant that runs on autopilot.

**Wanderlog** helps you decide what a trip should be. It is a collaborative, map-first planner: you and your travel companions save places, drag them into days, see everything plotted on a shared map, and edit the plan together in real time. It also includes group expense tracking with cost splitting, even on the free tier.

**Travel Stories** is a simpler, self-contained iPhone planner. One app holds the itinerary with maps, bookings, tasks, expenses against a budget, packing lists, and a travel diary with photos — all stored on your phone, working fully offline, with no account to create. Its pricing is the outlier: instead of a subscription, the full unlock is a one-time $1.99.

The pricing models are the sharpest dividing line, so let us get the verified numbers on the table first (checked July 2026, from each product's own pricing pages):

- **TripIt Pro: $49 per year**, subscription, 30-day free trial. A free tier exists.
- **Wanderlog Pro: $39.99 per year**, sold as an annual plan. A free tier exists.
- **Travel Stories Premium Lifetime: $1.99, one payment**, no recurring charge. The free version includes one complete trip.

## What does TripIt do best?

TripIt's core strength has not changed in over a decade, and that is a compliment: **email-forwarding itinerary automation**. You book flights, hotels, and cars wherever you like, forward the confirmation emails, and TripIt assembles a clean master itinerary with dates, times, confirmation numbers, and addresses. Connect your inbox and even the forwarding step disappears. For anyone juggling many bookings across many suppliers — especially frequent business travellers — this is genuinely hard to beat.

The free tier covers that core: itinerary building from emails, calendar sync, basic sharing, maps, and up to three document uploads per trip.

**TripIt Pro ($49/year)** is where the airline-day features live:

- Real-time flight alerts for delays, gate changes, and cancellations — often faster than the airline's own app
- Seat tracking that notifies you when a better seat opens up
- Fare monitoring that flags price drops on booked flights, which can mean refunds or credits
- Alternate flight search when things go wrong
- Airport walking directions to gates and baggage claims
- Reward-programme point tracking, and 25 document uploads per trip

**Where TripIt is weak:** it is an organiser, not a planner. There is no meaningful help deciding what to do at the destination, no budget tracking, no packing lists, and nothing resembling a travel journal. It organises the logistics layer of a trip and stops there. It also requires an account by design — the whole product is built around your email address.

## What does Wanderlog do best?

Wanderlog is the strongest app of the three for **collaborative planning on a map**. Every trip is a live document: you and your co-travellers add places from Google Maps data, arrange them into days, and see the whole plan plotted geographically. Everyone edits simultaneously, comments, and votes with their edits — it is the closest travel planning gets to working in a shared Google Doc.

The free tier is generous: unlimited places, live collaboration, the combined itinerary-and-map view, importing flight and hotel reservations, and group expense tracking with cost splitting between travellers.

**Wanderlog Pro ($39.99/year)** unlocks the power-user layer:

- Offline maps and offline access to your full plan
- Route optimisation that reorders a day's stops into the fastest sequence
- Automatic Gmail scanning to import bookings (its answer to TripIt's forwarding)
- Export to Google Maps, an AI trip assistant, unlimited attachments, and dark mode

**Where Wanderlog is weak:** the features most people would call essential on the road — offline access above all — sit behind the Pro paywall. Planning a trip on the free tier at home works beautifully; landing in a foreign city without data and discovering your maps need Pro is the classic Wanderlog complaint. It also requires an account (your trips live on Wanderlog's servers, which is what makes collaboration possible), and its journalling and memory-keeping features are thin.

## Where does Travel Stories fit?

Travel Stories makes a deliberately different set of trade-offs. It is not trying to out-automate TripIt or out-collaborate Wanderlog. It is built for the traveller who wants **one private, offline app for the whole arc of a trip** — planning it, living it, and remembering it — without a subscription or an account.

What it covers, all in one app:

- **Itinerary with maps** — day-by-day plans, bookings, and tasks, with departure reminders 1-30 days out and a Home Screen countdown widget
- **Budget and expenses** — set a trip budget, log spending as it happens, and (with the one-time unlock) see visual spending charts; the method from our guide on [how to budget for a trip](/blog/how-to-budget-for-a-trip/) maps directly onto it
- **Packing lists** — with over 100 built-in suggestions
- **Travel diary** — notes and photos captured per day, so the trip's memories live next to its plan
- **Share sheet capture** — send bookings, links, and tips into the right trip from any iOS app
- **Fully offline, no account** — everything stays on your phone; there is no server and no cloud sync, in 14 languages

The pricing is the headline: the free version includes **one trip with every feature**, and the Premium Lifetime unlock — unlimited trips, spending charts, itinerary export to Apple Calendar, trip sharing and exports — costs **$1.99 once**. If you plan a couple of trips a year and want the whole thing organised in one place without another recurring charge, you can [get Travel Stories on the App Store](https://apps.apple.com/app/id6756801168) and be done with pricing decisions permanently.

**Where Travel Stories is weak — honestly:** it is iPhone-only (iOS 17 or later, no Android or iPad version), so mixed-platform groups are out. There is no email-forwarding automation — you add bookings yourself, helped by the share sheet but not replaced by it. And because data stays on-device, there is no live collaborative editing; you share a trip as an export rather than a live document. If any of those three is a dealbreaker, it is a dealbreaker, and one of the other two apps will serve you better.

## How do the costs compare over time?

Subscriptions are small numbers that compound. Here is the straightforward maths for the paid tiers over a five-year horizon, at current prices:

| Timeframe | TripIt Pro ($49/yr) | Wanderlog Pro ($39.99/yr) | Travel Stories ($1.99 once) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 year | $49 | $39.99 | $1.99 |
| 3 years | $147 | $119.97 | $1.99 |
| 5 years | $245 | $199.95 | $1.99 |

Two honest caveats cut both ways. First, both TripIt and Wanderlog have real free tiers, and plenty of travellers never pay: TripIt Free still does email-forwarding itinerary building, and Wanderlog Free still does collaborative planning. The subscriptions buy the premium layer, not the core. Second, a subscription can be worth every cent — a frequent flyer whose TripIt Pro fare alert catches one price drop, or whose delay notification saves one missed connection, has covered the $49 several times over.

The comparison to make is with your own travel pattern: if you fly weekly, $49 a year for flight intelligence is cheap insurance. If you take two or three leisure trips a year and mostly need a place to keep the plan, the budget, and the memories, paying yearly for features you use twice is where the one-time $1.99 model wins. ## Which app should you choose?

**Choose TripIt if** you book many trips with many suppliers and want the itinerary to build itself. Its email automation remains the best in the category, and TripIt Pro's flight alerts are genuinely valuable for anyone who flies often. Accept that you will need an account, and that planning, budgeting, and journalling happen elsewhere.

**Choose Wanderlog if** you plan trips *with people* — friends, family, a partner who actually wants input. The shared map-and-itinerary view is the best collaborative planning experience of the three, and the free tier covers most group-planning needs. Budget for Pro if you want your maps offline when you land.

**Choose Travel Stories if** you are an iPhone traveller who wants planning, budgeting, packing, and a travel diary in a single offline app — and you would rather pay $1.99 once than sign up for anything. It will not forward-parse your emails or host a group editing session, but it covers the full life of a trip privately and works in airplane mode from day one.

And if none of the three fits exactly, the field is wider than this comparison — our round-up of the [best trip planner apps](/blog/best-trip-planner-apps/) covers the alternatives, including Google Maps-based workflows and spreadsheet planning.

## The bottom line

TripIt vs Wanderlog is not really a head-to-head — it is automation versus collaboration, and the right answer follows from how you travel. Travel Stories sits beside them as the third option the subscription model ignores: fewer bells, no account, everything offline, and a price that you pay once and forget. Verify the current prices before you commit (they were $49/year, $39.99/year, and $1.99 one-time as of July 2026), pick the model that matches your travel pattern, and let the app disappear into the background — which is what a good trip planner should do.
