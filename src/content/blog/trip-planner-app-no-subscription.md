## Why does every trip planner app want a subscription?

Because subscriptions are better for the developer, not for you. A one-time $30 sale is worth $30; a $40-a-year subscriber who forgets to cancel is worth $200 over five years. Investors value recurring revenue, app stores make renewals frictionless, and so almost every travel app that raised money or grew a team has moved to the subscription model.

The awkward part is that trip planning is not a subscription-shaped problem for most people. If you take one or two trips a year, you need the app intensely for a few weeks and not at all for the other eleven months — but the subscription charges you for all twelve. That mismatch is why "trip planner app no subscription" is a search people type in visible frustration, usually right after a renewal notification.

The good news: subscription-free options exist. The bad news: you have to read carefully to find them, because "free" and "one-time" are used loosely in this market. Here is what the major apps actually charge, verified in July 2026.

## Which trip planner apps charge a subscription?

The four biggest names in trip planning are all subscription products:

| App | Paid tier | Price (verified July 2026) | One-time option? |
|---|---|---|---|
| TripIt | TripIt Pro | $49/year | No |
| Wanderlog | Wanderlog Pro | ~$40/year (annual only) | No |
| Tripsy | Tripsy Pro | $59/year | Yes — $299 lifetime |
| Roadtrippers | Premium | $59.99/year or $19.99/month | No |

A few notes on each:

- **TripIt Pro ($49/year).** The free TripIt tier builds itineraries from forwarded confirmation emails and is usable on its own. Pro adds the flight-tracking layer: real-time delay alerts, alternative flight search, seat tracking. Useful if you fly constantly for work; hard to justify for two leisure trips a year. We compare it in depth in our [TripIt vs Wanderlog vs Travel Stories](/blog/tripit-vs-wanderlog-vs-travel-stories/) piece.
- **Wanderlog Pro (about $40/year).** Sold as an annual plan only — there is no monthly option — with a 7-day free trial. Regional pricing varies slightly. The free tier is the most generous of the big four, but see the trial-trap section below for the catch.
- **Tripsy Pro ($59/year).** The most expensive annual plan here, though Tripsy deserves credit for being one of the few to offer a lifetime alternative at $299. That is roughly five years of subscription paid up front — a real option, but a serious commitment for a trip planner.
- **Roadtrippers Premium ($59.99/year).** Focused on North American road trips. The free version is closer to a demo, with the trip-length and offline features held for Premium.

None of these are scams — they are real products with real features. The problem is subtler: the pricing patterns around them are designed to move you from "free" to "paying yearly" without a clear moment of decision.

## What are the trial traps to watch for?

Two patterns account for most of the money people spend on travel apps by accident.

### Trap 1: the auto-renewing trial

The standard flow: a 7-day (Wanderlog) or 30-day (TripIt Pro) free trial that requires you to start the subscription up front. The trial converts to a paid year automatically unless you cancel. You download the app in June while excited about a trip, the trip happens, and in July you are billed $40-49 for a year you will mostly not use.

The defence is mechanical, not moral — nobody remembers to cancel by willpower alone:

1. When you start any trial on iPhone, cancel it immediately in **Settings → [your name] → Subscriptions**. You keep full access until the trial expires, and it never converts.
2. If a trial requires payment details before you have seen the product, treat that as the price tag it is.

### Trap 2: "free" with the core paywalled

The second pattern is the free tier that works beautifully at home and fails abroad. Wanderlog is the clearest example: unlimited trips and collaborative planning cost nothing, but **offline access is a Pro feature**. You discover this standing in a foreign metro station with no signal, trying to load the itinerary you built — and the upgrade prompt appears at the exact moment you cannot comparison-shop. Route optimisation and exports sit behind the same paywall.

This is not unique to Wanderlog. Before trusting any free tier, ask one question: *does it still work with no internet, no account, and no payment?* If the answer is no, the free tier is a storefront, not a product.

### Trap 3: "lifetime" that is not what it sounds like

Smaller but worth knowing: some apps sell "lifetime" unlocks per feature pack rather than for the whole app, or price the lifetime option so high (Tripsy's is $299) that it functions mainly to make the subscription look cheap by contrast — a classic pricing anchor. A lifetime price is only meaningful if it is a number you would happily lose.

## Which trip planner apps have no subscription at all?

Ranked by how completely they avoid the subscription model:

1. **Travel Stories — free for one trip, $1.99 one-time for everything.** The free plan is one trip with *every* feature included: day-by-day itinerary with maps, bookings, tasks, expense tracking against a budget, packing lists with 100+ suggestions, and a travel diary with photos. It works fully offline with no account, because your data stays on the device — there is no server to sync to. Premium Lifetime is a $1.99 one-time purchase (19 DKK) that unlocks unlimited trips, visual spending charts, Apple Calendar export, and trip sharing. There is no subscription tier to upsell you to; if you plan more than one trip and want everything unlocked, the [Travel Stories app on the App Store](https://apps.apple.com/app/id6756801168) costs less than the airport coffee you will drink while using it. iPhone only, iOS 17 or later.
2. **TripIt (free tier).** If all you want is confirmation emails turned into a chronological itinerary, the free tier does that indefinitely without payment. You give up the Pro flight alerts, and TripIt is cloud-based, so it needs an account — but as a no-cost baseline it is honest.
3. **Wanderlog (free tier, with the offline caveat).** Genuinely capable for collaborative planning while you have signal. Fine for planning at home; risky as your only tool on the road, for the offline reason above.
4. **Tripsy (lifetime, $299).** The only big-name competitor selling a true buy-once option. The price makes sense only for very frequent travellers who are certain they will use it for many years.
5. **Apple Notes, Google Maps lists, and a spreadsheet.** The zero-cost stack. It works — people planned trips this way for decades — but you assemble everything by hand and nothing connects: the map does not know your schedule, the budget does not know your bookings. ## How do you check an app's real price before downloading?

Ninety seconds on the App Store page tells you almost everything:

- **Scroll to "In-App Purchases"** in the Information section. This list is the truth, whatever the screenshots say. Items marked per month or per year are subscriptions; a single fixed price is a one-time unlock.
- **Count the tiers.** One or two purchase options usually means simple pricing. Six-plus tiers with overlapping names ("Pro Monthly", "Pro Annual", "Premium Plus") usually means a pricing structure built for confusion.
- **Read the three most recent critical reviews.** Billing complaints cluster fast and reviewers name the exact pattern — "charged after cancelling", "trial converted without warning".
- **Check what the free tier excludes.** If reviews mention hitting a paywall mid-trip, believe them.

The pricing model also tells you something about incentives. A subscription app needs you to *keep needing it*, which rewards complexity and lock-in. A one-time-purchase app only needs you to be satisfied once, and a free tier with everything included is betting you will like the product enough to pay a small amount for more. That second bet is the one aligned with you actually enjoying your trip.

## Plan the trip, skip the renewal

The subscription model won trip planning by default, not by merit — for one or two trips a year it charges you for months of nothing.
