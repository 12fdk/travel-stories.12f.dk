## What should be on a travel checklist?

A complete travel checklist covers six groups — documents, home, money, health, tech, and packing — spread across four waves of time: two weeks out, one week out, the day before, and the day you leave. The timing matters as much as the items. Checking your passport the night before departure is a disaster; checking it two weeks out is a quick fix.

This page is the full checklist, ready to copy. Use it as-is for any trip, and skip the items that don't apply (no pets, no visa, domestic trip). If you're still in the planning stage — booking and itinerary building — start with the guide on [how to plan a trip step by step](/blog/how-to-plan-a-trip-step-by-step/) and come back here for the final two weeks.

Here's the whole thing at a glance:

| Wave | Focus | Time it takes |
|---|---|---|
| 2 weeks out | Long-lead items: documents, health, arrangements | ~1 hour, spread out |
| 1 week out | Money, tech, home logistics | ~1 hour |
| Day before | Packing, charging, confirmations | 2–3 hours |
| Day of | Final walkthrough and go | 20 minutes |

## Two weeks before you leave

This wave exists because some items simply can't be rushed. Everything here has a lead time measured in days or weeks, so it goes first.

**Documents**

- Check your passport — many destinations require validity 6 months past your return date
- Confirm whether your destination requires a visa, and apply if so (some take weeks — check as early as you can)
- Buy or review travel insurance; save the policy document and the emergency phone number
- Save digital copies of your passport, insurance, and all booking confirmations somewhere that works offline

**Health**

- Refill any prescriptions so you have enough for the whole trip plus a few spare days
- Check whether your destination recommends any vaccinations (some need time to take effect)
- Book any appointments you've been putting off — better before the trip than after

**Home**

- Arrange a pet sitter, kennel, or pet-friendly plans
- Arrange plant watering or move plants somewhere they'll survive
- Line up someone to check on the house if you'll be gone more than a week
- Request time off, set up handovers, and block your work calendar if you haven't already

**Money**

- Set your trip budget if you haven't — the guide on [how to budget for a trip](/blog/how-to-budget-for-a-trip/) covers the category split
- Check your card's foreign transaction fees so there are no statement surprises

**Packing**

- Start your packing list now — not to pack, but to find the gaps (adapter, swimwear, hiking shoes) while there's still time to buy or borrow
- Check baggage allowances for your specific fares, including the return flight

## One week before you leave

The middle wave: money, tech, and the home logistics that can't be done too early.

**Money**

- Notify your bank or card app of your travel dates if your bank wants it — an unannounced foreign charge can freeze a card
- Order or withdraw a small amount of local currency for arrival-day basics (transit, tips, a snack)
- Confirm you have at least two ways to pay that work abroad, kept in different bags

**Tech**

- Sort out your phone plan: roaming package, travel eSIM, or a local SIM plan for arrival
- Download offline maps for every city and region on your itinerary
- Download entertainment for the flight — movies, podcasts, books — while you're on fast home wifi
- Gather chargers, cables, a power bank, and the right plug adapters into one pouch
- Test the pouch: does every device you're bringing have its charger in there?

**Home**

- Pause or redirect mail and package deliveries
- Set light timers if you use them
- Plan meals to run the fridge down — stop buying perishables now
- Confirm the pet sitter and plant plan from last week are actually locked in

**Documents**

- Check in for your flight as soon as the airline allows, and save the boarding passes
- Re-confirm lodging bookings and note check-in times and addresses
- Print or screenshot anything critical — screenshots work when apps and signals don't

**Health**

- Assemble a small first-aid kit: pain relief, plasters, any personal essentials
- Put prescription meds in your hand luggage pile, in original packaging, never in checked baggage

**Packing**

- Do laundry so everything on the packing list is actually clean and available
- Finalize the packing list — the item-by-item version is in the [packing list for international travel](/blog/packing-list-for-international-travel/)

## The day before you leave

Packing day. Give it an unhurried two to three hours in the afternoon or evening, not a frantic hour at midnight. The difference between the two isn't the packing itself — it's what happens when you find a gap. At 4 p.m. a missing adapter is a quick errand; at midnight it's a problem you carry to the airport.

One rule shapes everything below: anything you cannot afford to lose travels in your hand luggage. Checked bags get delayed and occasionally lost. Medication, documents, electronics, and one change of clothes belong with you; everything replaceable can go under the plane.

**Packing**

- Pack everything, checking items off the list as they physically go in the bag
- Pack the essentials in hand luggage: medication, documents, chargers, one change of clothes in case checked bags are delayed
- Weigh your bag if you're near the airline's limit
- Leave a little empty space — trips have a way of coming home heavier

**Tech**

- Charge everything to full: phone, power bank, headphones, e-reader, camera
- Put the charged power bank in your hand luggage (it's not allowed in checked bags)

**Documents**

- Physically place passport, wallet, and phone in one spot by the door or in your personal bag
- Verify boarding passes open on your phone without a connection
- Verify tomorrow's flight time hasn't changed

**Home**

- Take out all trash, including the kitchen bin
- Run and empty the dishwasher; don't leave dishes in the sink
- Empty the fridge of anything that will expire; give food away rather than binning it
- Water the plants one last time
- Leave a key and a copy of your itinerary with someone you trust

**Money**

- Split cash and cards between two bags or pockets, so one loss isn't a total loss

**Health**

- Set two alarms for the morning, and go to bed at a sane hour — departure day is long

## The day you leave

Twenty minutes of calm checks, then out the door.

**The final walkthrough** — one slow lap of your home:

- All windows closed and locked
- Stove, oven, coffee maker, and iron off
- Thermostat set for an empty home
- Taps off; washing machine and dishwasher not mid-cycle
- Lights off (or timers on)
- All doors locked behind you

**The pocket check** — the four things that must be on your body, not in a checked bag:

- Passport (or ID for domestic trips)
- Phone
- Wallet with cards and cash
- Medication for the journey

**The logistics check**

- Confirm the flight status one last time before leaving home
- Leave earlier than feels necessary — international flights typically want you at the airport around 3 hours ahead, and security lines don't care about your schedule
- Know exactly how you're getting to the airport, with a backup if the first plan fails

If the walkthrough is done and the four pocket items are on you, you're ready. Everything else is either in the bag or genuinely optional.

## How do you make this checklist stick?

Three habits turn a one-time list into a system that gets better with every trip:

1. **Tick, don't skim.** A checklist you read is a suggestion; a checklist you check off is a guarantee. The physical act of marking items done is what catches the forgotten charger.
2. **Keep it with your trip, offline.** The moments you need the list — the taxi, the check-in desk, the gate — are the moments wifi disappears. Your checklist, boarding passes, and confirmations should all open with no signal.
3. **Add what you forgot.** Every trip teaches you one item. Forgot sunglasses in March? Add them to the list, and April's trip won't repeat it. A reused checklist compounds; a rewritten one starts from zero every time.
