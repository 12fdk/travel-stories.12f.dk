## Why bother with a post-trip checklist?

Because a trip does not end when the plane lands — it ends when the expenses are settled, the suitcase is empty, and the photos are somewhere you will actually find them. Skip that last stretch and you pay for it later: a double charge you never disputed, a reimbursement claim that expired, a travel kit you have to rebuild at the airport pharmacy, and 900 unsorted photos you never look at again.

The fix is the same one that works before departure: a short written checklist. You probably used a [pre-departure checklist](/blog/travel-checklist-before-leaving/) on the way out; this is its mirror image. Twelve items, four themes, one week. Here is the list.

## Finances: close out the money first

Money tasks go first because they are the only ones with real deadlines — refund windows, dispute periods, and expense-claim cutoffs all start ticking the moment you get home.

### 1. Log the final expenses

The last day of a trip is where expense tracking always breaks down: the airport lunch, the taxi home, the toll, the parking charge at your own airport. Log them within a day or two of landing, while the receipts are still in your jacket pocket and your memory. If you tracked spending during the trip, this closes the loop and gives you a true total — the number that makes your next budget honest instead of optimistic.

### 2. Check statements for double and surprise charges

About a week after you return, go through your card statements line by line against the trip. You are looking for three things: duplicate charges (hotels and car hire are the usual offenders), pending pre-authorisations that never released (fuel stations and hotel incidental holds), and conversion or foreign transaction fees you did not expect. Check again when the monthly statement closes, because some charges post late. Most banks give you 60-120 days to dispute a transaction — but only if you spot it.

### 3. Submit reimbursements and claims

If any part of the trip is claimable — work expenses, a delayed-flight compensation claim, a travel insurance claim, a VAT refund — do the paperwork this week. Every one of these gets harder with time: receipts fade, portals time out, and EU flight compensation claims are far easier to assemble while you still remember the gate changes. Set a hard rule: all claims submitted within seven days of landing.

While you are at it, cancel anything trip-specific that keeps billing: a temporary eSIM plan, an airport lounge day pass that auto-renews, a car-hire insurance add-on. These are small amounts, which is exactly why they survive unnoticed on statements for months.

## Practical: reset your gear and documents

These four tasks reset your household and your travel kit to "ready" — so the next trip starts from zero instead of from an archaeology dig.

### 4. Unpack systematically, within 24 hours

Empty the whole bag in one pass, sorting into three piles: laundry, put-away, and restock. The one-pass method matters because a half-unpacked suitcase hides problems — a damp swimsuit quietly ruining everything around it, a borrowed adapter that never gets returned, a passport still in the outside pocket. Twenty minutes, done. If you flew, this is also the moment to check that checked luggage came through undamaged — airlines typically require damage reports within seven days.

### 5. Run the trip laundry — all of it

Everything worn goes in the wash, and so does anything that shared a bag with a beach towel or hiking boots. Do it in the first day or two: stale trip laundry is the single most common reason an unpacked bag sits in the hallway for a week.

### 6. Restock the travel kit

Refill or replace whatever the trip used up: toiletries, medication, plasters, sunscreen, spare charging cables, the emergency snack. The best time to rebuild the kit is now, when you know exactly what ran out — not at 5 a.m. before the next departure. If you keep a standing [packing list](/blog/packing-list-for-international-travel/), tick the kit off against it so it is genuinely complete.

### 7. Check your passport's condition and expiry

Thirty seconds that can save a trip: check that your passport survived intact (water damage and torn pages can get you refused boarding) and look at the expiry date. Many countries require six months of validity beyond your travel dates, so if yours expires within a year, start the renewal now — not when the next trip is booked and the renewal queue is eight weeks long.

## Memory: preserve the trip while it is fresh

Memory tasks have a soft deadline: context. A week after you land, you still know which café that was and why the photo of a door was funny. A month later, you do not.

### 8. Organize the photos

Do two passes in the first week. Pass one: delete the obvious failures — blurry shots, duplicates, seventeen versions of the same sunset. Pass two: mark 20-50 favourites that tell the trip's story. That is enough; a curated album beats a perfect one that never gets made. The full method, including albums, backups, and what to do with a partner's photos, is in our guide to [organizing travel photos](/blog/how-to-organize-travel-photos/).

### 9. Finish the journal

If you kept a travel journal, write the final entry — the journey home, the best and worst moments, the meal you will still talk about in five years. If you did not keep one, a single retrospective page written this week captures more than nothing ever will: where you went, who was there, three highlights, one disaster. Our guide on [how to keep a travel journal](/blog/how-to-keep-a-travel-journal/) covers formats that take five minutes a day.

### 10. Archive the trip in one place

Bookings, tickets, the itinerary, the expense totals, the diary, the favourite photos — bring them together into a single trip record before they scatter across four apps and an email inbox. This is exactly what a trip app's history is for: in the free [Travel Stories app](https://apps.apple.com/app/id6756801168), the itinerary, expenses, packing list, and photo diary you built during the trip already sit together as one finished, offline record — closing the trip is simply a matter of writing the last diary entry.

## Forward-looking: bank the lessons

The final two items take ten minutes and are the difference between getting better at travelling and making the same mistakes annually.

### 11. Note what you would pack and plan differently

While the trip is fresh, write down the specifics: the jacket you never wore, the second pair of shoes you did not need, the adapter you had to buy, the museum that needed pre-booking, the neighbourhood you wish you had stayed in. Attach these notes to your packing list and planning notes, not to a random document — next trip, you want them to surface automatically.

### 12. Start the next trip's wishlist

Every trip generates ideas for the next one: a place a local recommended, a town you passed through too quickly, a country a fellow traveller would not stop talking about. Capture them now in a wishlist — a note, a saved list, or a draft trip. It costs nothing, and six months later it turns "where should we go?" from a blank page into a shortlist. When one of them becomes real, our guide on [how to plan a trip step by step](/blog/how-to-plan-a-trip-step-by-step/) picks up from there.

## The one-week schedule

Twelve items sounds like a lot; spread across a week it is not:

| Day | Tasks |
|---|---|
| Day 1 | Unpack (4), start laundry (5), log final expenses (1) |
| Day 2-3 | Restock the travel kit (6), check passport (7), photo pass one (8) |
| Day 4-5 | Submit reimbursements and claims (3), photo pass two (8), finish the journal (9) |
| Day 6-7 | Check statements (2), archive the trip (10), lessons and wishlist (11, 12) |

No single day takes more than about half an hour. By the weekend, the money is settled, the gear is reset, the memories are kept, and the next trip already has a head start — which is the entire point of a post-trip checklist: the trip ends deliberately, instead of just trailing off.
